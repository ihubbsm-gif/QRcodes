ANTIGRAVITY CONE

(Centre of Gravity & Stability)

Academic Information

Class: XI

Topic: Centre of Gravity (CG)

Concepts: Physics, Motion, Stability

Learning Outcomes
  • Explore how the antigravity cone creates the illusion of defying gravity.
  • Investigate the physics principles and visual effects that contribute to this phenomenon.
  • Understand the concept of Centre of Gravity (CG).
  • Analyze how changes in the centre of gravity affect the motion and stability of objects.
Principle
  • The "Anti-Gravity Cone" is a fun science project that demonstrates an important physics concept: Centre of Gravity (CG).
  • Although it appears that the cone rolls uphill against gravity, there is actually no anti-gravity involved.
  • Every object has a Centre of Gravity (CG) — the point where all of its weight appears to be concentrated.
  • Imagine a teeter-totter; the CG of the people sitting on each side determines which side moves down.
  • The antigravity cone works by changing the position of its centre of gravity during motion.

CoG = (ΣD × W) / ΣW

  • The location of the centre of gravity can be found by multiplying the distance (D) by the weight (W), adding all these values together, and dividing the result by the total weight.
Working
  • The model typically consists of two cones joined together at their bases.
  • This creates a shape with a wider end and a narrower end.
  • The cone is placed on a specially designed track having a slight incline.
  • As the cone moves along the track, the wider end gains more contact with the track surface.
  • This causes the centre of gravity to shift lower.
  • Even though the cone appears to be rolling uphill, its centre of gravity is actually moving downward.
  • The cone continues to follow the slope of the track while creating the illusion of moving against gravity.
Participate & Observe
  • Place the cone at the lower end of the track and release it gently.
  • Observe the direction in which the cone moves.
  • Notice that the cone appears to roll upward along the inclined track.
  • Observe the wider and narrower ends of the cone during motion.
  • Identify how the contact points between the cone and the track change.
  • Discuss why the cone appears to move against gravity.
  • Relate the movement to the changing position of the centre of gravity.
  • Compare the observed motion with your initial expectations.
Applications
  • Understanding the centre of gravity is crucial in engineering and design.
  • Engineers use CG concepts while designing skyscrapers, bridges, bicycles, vehicles, and machines and equipment.
  • Proper CG placement helps ensure stability, balance, safety, and optimal performance.
  • The model demonstrates how seemingly magical phenomena can be explained using scientific principles.
  • It provides a fun and interactive way to learn about physics and the behaviour of objects in motion.
