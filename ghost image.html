GHOST IMAGE

(Persistence of Vision & Optical Illusion)

Academic Information

Class: VIII onwards

Topic: Persistence of Vision & Visual Perception

Concepts: Optical Illusions, Depth Perception, Motion Cues

Learning Outcomes
  • Explain how depth perception contributes to the rotating ghost illusion.
  • Understand the role of motion cues in perceiving a complete 3D figure.
  • Learn the concept of persistence of vision.
  • Apply the principles of optical illusions in design and animation.
  • Develop an understanding of visual perception and image formation.
Science Involved
  • The phenomenon is based on the principle of persistence of vision.
  • The human eye retains an image for a short time after it disappears.
  • The brain combines rapidly changing images into a continuous perception.
  • This creates the illusion of a complete image and smooth motion.
Working and Observation
  • Observe the rapidly rotating half-image of the demon's face.
  • Notice how the brain attempts to complete the missing half of the image.
  • The persistence of vision creates an illusion of completeness.
  • Rapid rotation may produce blur and distortion of facial features.
  • The brain may perceive phantom features that are not actually present.
  • The fast-moving image may create a feeling of dizziness or disorientation.
  • The illusion often generates fascination and curiosity about visual perception.
Applications
  • Sparkler's Trail Effect: The glowing trail remains visible due to persistence of vision.
  • Thaumatrope: Two separate images appear as a single combined image when spun rapidly.
  • Film and Animation: Rapidly displayed frames merge into smooth continuous motion.
  • Visual Effects: Used to create realistic motion and animation effects.
  • Educational Demonstrations: Helps explain persistence of vision and optical illusions.
